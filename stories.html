My Story

How it happened

I was in fifth grade when a cup of scalding hot coffee was accidentally knocked over, spilling down my entire right leg.

Everything happened quickly. I was rushed to the hospital and spent the night in the emergency department. At the time, I did not fully understand what was happening. I only knew that I had been badly burned and that recovery was going to take time.

Recovery

The very next day was the start of Outdoor School, a week I had been looking forward to for months. I did not want my injury to keep me from going, so I went anyway.

Every day, my dad drove hours just to meet me so he could change my bandages before driving home again. Looking back now, I realize how much that meant. At the time, I just wanted to feel as normal as possible and not let the injury stop me from experiencing something I had been excited about.

Healing did not end when the wound closed. Over the following years, I continued returning to the burn clinic for follow-up appointments and laser treatments to help reduce the scar. I still remember how painful those treatments were.

Living with a scar

Although my scar gradually became less noticeable over time, it never completely disappeared. Even today, the skin on my leg looks and feels different than it did before the injury.

Growing up, I often felt self-conscious about it. I worried that people would notice my scar or ask questions, and there were times when I wished my leg looked the way it had before. Looking back, I realize that recovering from a burn is not just about healing physically. It is also about learning to become comfortable in your own skin again.

What I wish I had known

When I was younger, I thought recovery meant that one day everything would go back to exactly the way it had been before. Instead, I learned that healing can look different for everyone.

I also wish I had known that it was okay to feel self-conscious. Those feelings are normal, and they do not last forever. Over time, I stopped seeing my scar as something I needed to hide. Today, it reminds me of what I went through, but it no longer defines how I see myself.

Why I am sharing my story

For many years, my burn was simply something that had happened to me. It was not until I became interested in medicine that I began thinking about the experiences of other burn survivors and their families.

I created this project because I hope no child or parent feels like they have to navigate recovery alone. My story is only one of many, and every burn journey is different. My hope is that by sharing experiences and connecting families with trusted resources, this project can offer reassurance, practical guidance, and a reminder that healing continues long after leaving the hospital.
